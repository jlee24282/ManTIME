It directed the department to come up with a plan for &quot;operational control&quot; of the border. That could end up meaning a few dozen miles of new fencing or a few hundred, but most likely not a wall stretching the 2,000 miles from the Gulf Coast to the Pacific or new construction along the parts of the border that already have extensive fortification. &#10;&#10;Similarly, a second order issued the same day on sanctuary cities allows the administration &quot;at its discretion&quot; to cut off an unspecified range of federal funds from certain cities that &quot;willfully refuse to comply&quot; with federal immigration law. That could sweep in a lot of localities or very few, depending on how big a confrontation the new president eventually decides he wants. &#10;&#10;The exception so far - the one order that had major immediate consequences - was the temporary ban on granting entry visas to residents of seven majority-Muslim nations in the Mideast and North Africa, which stopped travelers at airports worldwide. That order, especially the favored status it provided to Christians and other non-Muslims, brought threats of lawsuits and outraged denunciations by Trump's opponents. &#10;&#10;The lack of protest from all but a couple of congressional Republicans, however, reinforces what polling during the campaign season showed: Even a full ban on Muslim immigration, which would be more sweeping than Friday's order, has considerable support among Trump's voters. &#10;&#10;In other areas, the administration has so far steered away from actions that would have carried big political risk. &#10;&#10;Most notably, Trump has not ended former President Barack Obama's program to exempt from deportation young immigrants who came to the U.S. illegally as children. &#10;&#10;Through the week, the administration continued to accept applications for the program, known as DACA, for Deferred Action for Childhood Arrivals.
