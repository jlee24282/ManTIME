the night of April 17, 1969, Dick Tinkham sat courtside for a playoff game many expected the Indiana Pacers to lose. &#10; &#10;The Pacers were two years into existence, facing a Game 7 against the Kentucky Colonels after falling behind 3-1 in the series. About 11,000 fans packed into Indiana State Fairgrounds Coliseum for the game, cheering and hollering the entire time. &#10; &#10;Except there was Tinkham, who co-founded both the ABA and the Pacers franchise, watching more nervously than everyone else because he knew a secret: The Pacers were a day-to-day operation with no long-term existence guaranteed. &#10; &#10;“We’d raised some money, we’d spent the money, and we realized we had to go pretty far in the playoffs to continue to operate because the team, early on, was not a raving investment vehicle for people,” Tinkham said. “I’m sitting there realizing, ‘What happens (if we lose)?  We don’t have a plan. You end on a playoff loss, who wants it?’ We honestly didn’t have a long-range plan at that point. We just figured we had to win.” &#10; &#10;Bob Netolicky, an ABA Pacers great, didn’t know the consequences of the game as a 26-year-old ballplayer out of Drake. He remembers Game 7 fondly for scoring 32 points and grabbing 16 rebounds, pushing the Pacers to their first-ever playoff series victory. &#10; &#10;But he didn't know that a loss might have meant the end of the Pacers. &#10; &#10;“I think, 'Thank God we didn’t know what was on the line,' because I don’t know if it would have affected the players or not,” Netolicky said, adding: “If we lost that game, which we were supposed (to do), the Pacers would fold.” &#10; &#10;Perhaps less surprising now that the Pacers are celebrating their 50th season as a franchise — they were founded Feb. 2, 1967 as an original member of the ABA — the winning didn’t stop after they beat the Colonels, 120-111, that night in 1969. &#10; &#10;The Pacers eliminated the Miami Floridians 4-1 in the next round, then suffered a 4-1 series loss in the ABA finals to the Oakland Oaks. But they had saved the franchise. Indiana won its first championship the following season and reached the finals five times total, adding two more titles in its nine years in the ABA. &#10; &#10;But nearly 48 years later, Tinkham and Netolicky still talk about the night the franchise won its first playoff series as the moment that changed everything for the organization. &#10;  &#10;Dick Tinkham (1995 File Photo by Frank Espich), original owner of the Indiana Pacers  (Photo: FILE PHOTO) &#10;  &#10;“The defining moment was one in which we understood we had to win to stay in business,” Tinkham said. “Because there were other owners in the league who had a lot more money than we had who weren’t winning and they were folding. The box office was not giving them enough money to operate on.” &#10; &#10;Tinkham doesn’t sugarcoat the early days. The way he tells it, on that day in February 1967 when the official announcement was made that Indianapolis would once again house a professional basketball team, none of the owners knew much about the basketball industry. &#10; &#10;Netolicky refers to the years in the late 1960s as the sport’s “dark ages,” where “the NBA was about as popular as night bowling.” &#10; &#10;It was a different time, long before the days of superstars such as Paul George making roughly $240,000 per game.
